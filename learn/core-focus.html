🧭 Core Focus

Why your business exists (Purpose) + what you do better than anyone (Niche).

What this is

Two short statements that anchor every strategic decision: Purpose (why you exist) and Niche (what you do better than anyone else for whom). Both are discovered, not invented — they live in the work you already love doing.

Why it matters

Without Purpose, teams default to “make money” — which doesn’t inspire decisions. Without Niche, you accept every opportunity that walks in the door and your edge erodes.

Done well, Core Focus is the filter: every quarter, every hire, every product decision passes through it. Done badly (or skipped), the business drifts.

The exercise (~70 mins, leadership team)

  1. Quiet time, 5 min. Each leader silently writes their answer to two prompts:
    • Why does this business really exist? What’s the deeper reason?
    • What do we do better than anyone else? And for whom?
  2. Share without discussion, 10 min. One person at a time reads their answers. No interruptions, no cross-talk. Capture every answer on the board, unedited.
  3. Stir the pot, 15 min. Facilitator: “What patterns do you see? What sounds half-right? What sounds wrong?” Cross-talk now welcome.
  4. Work toward 80% consensus, 30 min. Iterate the Purpose statement, then the Niche statement. Don’t shoot for 100% perfect — 80% agreement, then move on. Perfect is the enemy of done.
  5. Test, 10 min. Ask each leader separately to repeat both statements back. If everyone says roughly the same thing, you’ve landed. If three different versions come out, keep working.

The 4 Purpose archetypes (when stuck)

Most great Purpose statements fall into one of four shapes. If you’re stuck, try each:

  • Solving — “We exist to solve X.”
  • Helping — “We exist to help X people do Y.”
  • Building — “We exist to build a great company that does X.”
  • Winning — “We exist to be the best in the world at X.”

Reference: Jim Collins, Good to Great, chapter 5 (the Hedgehog Concept) — the intersection of what you’re passionate about, what you can be best at, and what drives the business.

The Niche test — “client × problem × outcome”

Finish this sentence:

“When [specific client] has [specific problem], we are uniquely good at [specific outcome].”

If you can’t name the client, problem, or outcome specifically, your niche is too wide. Sharpen each blank.

Common pitfalls

  • Writing Purpose as what you do (“We provide coaching services”). Purpose is the why. The what lives elsewhere.
  • “We help everyone do everything” — a niche of nothing. The more specific, the more powerful.
  • Chasing “shiny stuff” outside the niche. Every opportunity that fits poorly degrades the edge a little.
  • Inventing instead of discovering. Look at the work you already love doing — the work where you naturally outperform. The niche is in there.
  • Trying to land it in one sitting. Many leadership teams need three rounds across two weeks.
Coach’s tip
Print Purpose + Niche on a card at every desk. Use them as a filter at every quarterly session — “are we still doing the work that fits?” If not, the niche has drifted. Refocus before it costs you a year.
