👥 Leadership Team

The 4–8 people who lead the business, and what each one is accountable for.

What this is

A simple chart that names every function the business needs to run, plus the single person accountable for each. Names the gaps where no-one fits. Most growth problems are accountability problems — this is the tool that fixes them.

Why it matters

When two people half-own a function, no-one owns it. When a function has no owner, important things don’t get done. When the chart is fuzzy, the team duplicates work and drops balls. Writing it down forces clarity that most leadership teams have avoided for years.

Two ground rules (set these BEFORE anyone speaks)

  1. Look forward 6–12 months, not back. The chart describes what the business will need, not what it has today. Otherwise you’ll just describe the current dysfunction.
  2. No people yet. First we agree on structure. Putting names on too early makes people defend their seat instead of fixing the structure. Detach from the role — assume someone could be out of a job.

The exercise (~90 mins)

  1. Three major functions (10 min). Every business has these. Draw three boxes:
    • Sales & Marketing — bringing in customers
    • Operations — delivering for customers
    • Finance & Admin — running the business
  2. The Integrator role (10 min). Above the three boxes, draw a fourth: the Integrator — the person who runs day-to-day, manages the leadership team, removes blockers, owns execution. (Optional: a Visionary seat above for founders who set direction but don’t manage.)
  3. Five roles per box (20 min). For each major function, list the 5 most important roles inside it. Examples for Operations: head of delivery, head of product, head of customer success, head of engineering, head of IT. Don’t list titles — list accountabilities (what they own).
  4. Add the LMA rule (5 min). Any seat that has people reporting to it automatically takes on Role #1: Lead, Manage, Accountability — they lead the function strategically, manage the team day-to-day, and own the outcome.
  5. Names in seats (20 min). Now write a single name against each seat. Apply the fit test — the person needs all three:
    • They understand it — the role makes sense to them intellectually
    • They want it — genuinely motivated, not just willing
    • They have the capacity — time, skill, emotional bandwidth
    If two leaders fit a seat, pick one. If no-one fits, write [OPEN] — that’s a hiring need; surface it now, don’t hide it.
  6. The 3 yes questions (15 min). You’re done when the team says YES to all three:
    1. Is this the right structure?
    2. Are all the right people in the right seats?
    3. Does each person have enough time to do their job well?
    If any are no, capture the gap as an issue for the issues list.

Common pitfalls

  • Listing titles instead of accountabilities. “Operations Manager” is a title; “Owns delivery cost per client” is an accountability.
  • Two names on one seat. If two are accountable, no-one is. Pick.
  • Hiding from gaps. Write [OPEN] rather than quietly deleting a function the business needs.
  • Letting “willing” substitute for fit. All three of understands / wants / capacity have to be true.
  • Going people-first. Always structure first — otherwise you preserve the dysfunction.
Coach’s tip
Share the chart with every new hire on day one. They should know who owns what before they finish their first week. Update it formally every quarter at the planning session — the structure shifts as you grow, but only on purpose.
