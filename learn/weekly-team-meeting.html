🗓️ Weekly Team Meeting

Same day, same time, same agenda — every week. The cadence that holds everything together.

What this is

A 60–90 minute meeting, same day and time every week, with a fixed agenda. The heartbeat of the operating system. Without it, everything drifts.

Why it matters

Without a weekly drumbeat, issues fester, goals drift, and the team coordinates by ad-hoc Slack messages. The weekly meeting puts a 7-day procrastination cap on every decision — there’s only so long anyone can put something off when it’s coming round again Monday.

The 5-point cadence rule

Memorize this. Break one rule and the discipline erodes. Break two and the meeting dies.

  1. Same day every week
  2. Same time every week
  3. Same agenda every week
  4. Start on time
  5. End on time

The agenda (90 minutes total)

  1. Segue — 5 min. Each person: one personal good news, one business good news. Sets the tone — people show up grumpy and leave smiling.
  2. Numbers review — 5 min. Each owner reports their scorecard number: on track / off track. NO DISCUSSION. Off-track numbers drop down to the issues list.
  3. Goals review — 5 min. Each owner: on track / off track / done. NO DISCUSSION. Off-track goals drop down to the issues list.
  4. Customer & Team Highlights — 5 min. Brief good/bad reports — wins to celebrate, concerns to surface. NO DISCUSSION. Anything bigger drops down.
  5. Todo list — 5 min. Review last week’s todos. Done / not done. 90% should be dropping off the list each week. If they aren’t, you have a follow-through problem — add it as an issue.
  6. Issues — Discuss & Resolve — 60 min. 2/3 of the meeting goes here.
    • 30 seconds to prioritize the top 3 issues — gut feel, no debate
    • Work them one at a time using the 3-step protocol (see the Issues guide)
    • 1–2 week action items go on the todo list with an owner + date
    • Unresolved issues carry to next week
  7. Conclude — 5 min.
    • Review todos created in this meeting
    • Cascading messages — for each: who needs to know? When? How?
    • Each person rates the meeting 1–10 with one sentence why

End on time. Always. Items that remain go back on the list — the discipline of ending on time is what protects the discipline of starting on time.

The “drop it down” principle

Everything in the front half of the meeting (Numbers, Goals, Highlights, Todos) is reporting only. The moment someone starts to discuss, the facilitator says “drop it down” — meaning, add it to the issues list and discuss in section 6. This is what keeps the front 25 minutes fast and the meeting on time.

Common pitfalls

  • Letting reporting items become discussion items. Use “drop it down” mercilessly.
  • A todo list where 50% is dropping off. That’s a discipline problem, not a meeting problem.
  • Skipping the conclude. The rating is how you find out the meeting is broken — without it, you’ll keep running a bad meeting.
  • Skipping the meeting because “this week we’re too busy”. You’re busy because you skipped it last time.
  • Wrong people in the room. Leadership team only — 4–8 people. Bigger groups produce worse meetings.
Coach’s tip
If the meeting rates below 8 for three weeks running, the agenda or the attendees need adjusting. Don’t ignore the signal — a low-rated meeting is the leading indicator that the operating system is fraying.
