🎯 Targets

Your 10-year vision, 3-year outlook, and 1-year plan — so today's work has a finish line.

What this is

Your destination at three time horizons (10 / 3 / 1 years), with concrete numbers. Together they answer “Where are we going?” The further out, the bigger and rougher the vision; the closer in, the more specific the commitments.

Why it matters

“Grow the business” isn’t a target — you can’t tell if you’re winning. Numbers at 10 / 3 / 1 give every decision a benchmark. They also force the team to agree on where the business is going — most leadership teams privately disagree by 30%+ until they write it down.

The exercise — 10-Year Vision (~30 min)

  1. Take a shot over the bow. Someone proposes a timeframe (most pick 10 years — range is 5–30) and a revenue number that feels big but possible. Don’t debate yet; just put a stake in the ground.
  2. SMART filter. Pressure-test the draft: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound. Adjust until it passes.
  3. Write it as if already true. “By 2036 we are…” — team size, client base, geography, what you’re known for, what life feels like as the founder.
  4. Read aloud. Tune until every leader can say it without flinching.

The exercise — 3-Year Outlook (~60 min)

This is where the abstract vision starts hitting the ground. By now you know who you are (Core Values), what you love doing (Core Focus), where you’re going (10-Year), and how (Marketing Strategy). The 3-Year paints the picture of what that looks like in 36 months.

  1. Set the future date — 3 years from today, tied to calendar or fiscal year-end.
  2. Revenue — shot over the bow, then debate. Someone proposes the revenue number. Then the team challenges: does this match the growth rate the business can absorb? Does the financial model support it? Record the agreed number.
  3. Profit — shot over the bow, then debate. Same process. Record.
  4. One Measurable. Pick one number besides revenue/profit that defines the size and scope of the business as it’ll be. Examples: number of units shipped, market share %, number of widgets, customer count. Shot over the bow → debate → record.
  5. 5 quiet minutes: each leader silently writes their vision of what the business looks like in bullet form. Cover both company and division/department level. Useful prompts:
    • # of employees (and whether the org chart is filled with the right people in the right seats)
    • # of clients / customers
    • Locations — cities, regions, offices
    • New products or services launched
    • Marketing and sales efforts
    • What your own role looks like in the business at that time
  6. One at a time onto the board, no discussion. Each person reads one bullet. Captured without debate.
  7. Now debate, refine, K/K/Tweak. Walk every bullet. Keep, kill, or tweak. Aim for 5–15 surviving bullets. Err on the side of leaving something on — better to over-paint than under-paint.
  8. The see-it / want-it / believe-it test. Everyone closes their eyes. Facilitator: “You are sitting in your annual planning session three years from now.” Then reads each bullet aloud. After every bullet, ask:
    • Do you see it?
    • Do you want it?
    • Do you believe it’s possible?
    All three need YES from everyone. If anyone hesitates, the picture isn’t clear enough — keep going.
  9. Each person shares the role they see themselves in. No discussion — just their own picture. Surfaces misalignment fast.

The exercise — 1-Year Plan (~60 min)

  1. Future date = end of next fiscal or calendar year.
  2. Revenue, Profit, Measurables — shot over the bow, then debate, then record. Each one. These must be stepping stones to the 3-Year, not arbitrary numbers.
  3. 5 quiet minutes. Each leader silently answers one question:

    “Looking at the 3-Year Outlook, the Issues List, and the Org Chart — what are the 3–7 most important things that must get done by [date] to be on track for the 3-Year?”

  4. One-at-a-time onto the board. Someone proposes one goal. Discuss, debate, refine. If it’s a goal, write it on the board and go to the next. If not, throw it out and go to the next. Don’t batch — one at a time.
  5. SMART filter every surviving goal. 3–7 SMART goals. Hopefully 3. Worst case 7.
  6. Closing question:

    “If we are sitting here on [target date] with this revenue, this profit, these measurables, and we have achieved goal #1 — [name it], goal #2 — [name it], goal #3 — [name it]… will that have been a great year in the life of this company?”

    Read every goal aloud. Need YES from every leader to move on. If anyone hesitates, something’s missing — or one of the goals is wrong.

  7. Budget-support check. Does the budget support this plan? If not, fix one or the other now — not in May.
  8. Cascade. Each leader takes a goal back to their function and breaks it into departmental goals before the next weekly meeting.

Common pitfalls

  • Vague numbers (“more”, “grow”). If it’s not a dollar amount or count, it won’t drive behaviour.
  • A 1-Year Plan with 30 items. Pick 3–7. Everything else is noise.
  • 3-Year Outlook that’s just last year × 1.1. Be bolder — the see-it/want-it/believe-it test surfaces this every time.
  • Skipping the “does the budget support this?” test. The plan reads great on Day 1 and falls apart by Q2.
  • Setting it and not reviewing every quarter. The 1-Year Plan is the most-revisited doc in the business.
  • Batch-debating the 1-Year goals. One at a time — otherwise weak goals survive in the noise.
Coach’s tip
Review the 1-Year Plan at every quarterly session. If a goal is 25% done at the 50% mark, replan — don’t just hope. Either add resources, cut scope, or accept the goal won’t ship and protect the others.
