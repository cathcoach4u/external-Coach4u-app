🎯 Targets

Your 10-year vision, 3-year outlook, and 1-year plan — so today's work has a finish line.

What this is

Your destination at three time horizons (10 / 3 / 1 years), with concrete numbers. Together they answer “Where are we going?” The further out, the bigger and rougher the vision; the closer in, the more specific the commitments.

Why it matters

“Grow the business” isn’t a target — you can’t tell if you’re winning. Numbers at 10 / 3 / 1 give every decision a benchmark. They also force the team to agree on where the business is going — most leadership teams privately disagree by 30%+ until they write it down.

The exercise — 10-Year Vision (~30 min)

  1. Take a shot over the bow. Someone proposes a timeframe (most pick 10 years — range is 5–30) and a revenue number that feels big but possible. Don’t debate yet; just put a stake in the ground.
  2. SMART filter. Pressure-test the draft: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound. Adjust until it passes.
  3. Write it as if already true. “By 2036 we are…” — team size, client base, geography, what you’re known for, what life feels like as the founder.
  4. Read aloud. Tune until every leader can say it without flinching.

The exercise — 3-Year Outlook (~30 min)

  1. Set the future date — 3 years from today.
  2. Revenue, Profit, Measurables. Pick numbers that, if you hit them, would clearly put you on track for the 10-Year Vision.
  3. Describe the business as it’ll be. 5–10 bullet points: team size, capabilities, geography, recurring vs. one-off mix, biggest customer concentration. Keep / Kill / Combine each bullet.
  4. Close-your-eyes test. Facilitator: “Imagine you’re sitting in this room 3 years from now. Look around. Do you all see it?” Everyone has to say YES. If they hesitate, the picture isn’t clear enough — keep going.

The exercise — 1-Year Plan (~30 min)

  1. Future date = end of next fiscal year.
  2. Revenue + Profit. These are stepping stones to the 3-Year — not arbitrary.
  3. 3–7 goals for the year. Each needs a measurable result. (“Improve onboarding” doesn’t qualify; “Reduce time-to-first-value from 14 to 7 days” does.) Each has an owner.
  4. Closing test #1: “If we achieve this plan, will it be a good year?” If anyone hesitates, dig in.
  5. Closing test #2: “Does the budget support this?” The plan and the budget must agree. If they don’t, fix it now — not in May.
  6. Cascade. Each leader takes one piece into their function and breaks it into departmental goals before the next weekly meeting.

Common pitfalls

  • Vague numbers (“more”, “grow”). If it’s not a dollar amount or count, it won’t drive behaviour.
  • A 1-Year Plan with 30 items. Pick 3–7. Everything else is noise.
  • 3-Year Outlook that’s just last year × 1.1. Be bolder — the close-eyes test surfaces this.
  • Skipping the budget-support test. The plan reads great on Day 1 and falls apart by Q2 because you couldn’t afford it.
  • Setting it and not reviewing every quarter. The 1-Year Plan is the most-revisited doc in the business.
Coach’s tip
Review the 1-Year Plan at every quarterly session. If a goal is 25% done at the 50% mark, replan — don’t just hope. Either add resources, cut scope, or accept the goal won’t ship and protect the others.
