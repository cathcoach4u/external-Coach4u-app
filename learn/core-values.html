⭐ Core Values

The 5–8 non-negotiables that guide every hire, decision, and client interaction.

What this is

Your core values are the 3–7 timeless guiding principles that define your culture — who you are and what you stand for. They're not what you wish you were. They're not the things every business says ("integrity, honesty"). They're the behaviours your best people already show.

Core values aren't chosen. They're discovered by looking at the people you'd happily clone, and noticing what they have in common.

Why it matters

Values you don't define still drive your business — they just drive it inconsistently. Made explicit, they become the basis for the five most important people decisions: hire, fire, review, reward, and recognise. If a value can't be used in those five ways, it's not a value — it's a preference.

The exercise — how to discover them

Block out 90–120 minutes with your leadership team. Whiteboard or large pad on the wall. The whole process is below; work through it in order.

  1. Quiet time, 5 minutes. Each person silently thinks of three people — if you had 100 of them, you'd take over your industry. Don't pick the leadership team — look one layer down inside your business, then outside (other businesses, ex-colleagues). Write the names on the board.
  2. List the characteristics, 15 minutes. For each person on the board, list the qualities they have that made you pick them — specific behaviours, not job skills. ("Always picks up the phone." "Owns the outcome, not the task." "Tells me the bad news first.") The whiteboard will fill up — that's the point.
  3. First pass — Keep, Kill, Combine, 20 minutes. Walk the list. For each characteristic, the team agrees: Keep (this matters), Kill (it doesn't), or Combine (this is the same idea as that one, phrased differently). Aim to land at 10–15 surviving themes.
  4. Take a 10-minute break. Eyes off the list. Coffee. Come back fresh.
  5. Apply the three traps (next section), 15 minutes. Read the trap descriptions below as a team. Mark each surviving theme: is it Accidental? Aspirational? Permission-to-play? Mark them as you go.
  6. Second pass — Keep, Kill, Combine, 30 minutes. Cut the traps. Cut anything you couldn't use to hire / fire / review / reward / recognise. Keep going until you have 3–7 values. Less is more — harder to remember, easier to ignore.
  7. Test them, 15 minutes. See the next section. If your draft values don't pass the test, go back to step 6.

The three value traps

From Patrick Lencioni's Make Your Values Mean Something (Harvard Business Review, 2002) — the three failure modes that turn values into wall art:

  1. Accidental values. They emerged spontaneously over time, often from a particular subset of the team or an earlier era of the business. They describe who you've ended up being, not who you're choosing to be. Test: "If our top performers actively pushed back on this value, would I change it?" If yes, it's accidental.
  2. Aspirational values. You wish your business had them, but right now, frankly, it doesn't. Aspirational values are the most dangerous — the team sees the gap between the words and the behaviour, and trust erodes. Test: "Can I name three people who currently fail this value, and are we doing something about it?" If you can't name three, or you're not doing something, it's aspirational.
  3. Permission-to-play values. Generic table-stakes that every business claims — integrity, honesty, respect, professionalism. They're necessary, but they don't differentiate. They won't help you pick between two good hires. Test: "Could a direct competitor say this with a straight face?" If yes, it's permission-to-play. Either cut it or sharpen it ("Brutally direct, never mean" beats "Honesty").

Test what you've got

Before you commit, run two tests against the draft values:

  1. The People Test. List 3–5 real people on your team. Score each of them, against each draft value, on a 1–5 scale. Real values differentiate: your stars score high, your bottom-end scores low, and you can predict the scores before you write them. If everyone scores the same, the value isn't doing any work — it's permission-to-play. Cut or sharpen.
  2. The Five-Use Test. For each value, ask: would I actually use this to…
    • … hire? (Reject a strong candidate who fails it.)
    • … fire? (Let go of a top performer who violates it.)
    • … review? (Use it in a quarterly conversation.)
    • … reward? (Promote or pay for it.)
    • … recognise? (Call it out in a team meeting.)
    If you can't honestly say yes to all five, it's not a value. Demote it to "operating principle" or cut.

You'll know they're the right ones when reading the list back to the leadership team feels — uncomfortably — true.

Common pitfalls

  • Skipping the "100 of them" prompt. If you start the exercise by free-listing values, you'll get the permission-to-play list every time. Start with real people. The values emerge from the patterns.
  • Doing it solo. Values discovery works as a team conversation. Try it alone and you'll write what's in your head, not what the leadership team actually agrees on. The disagreement is where the value lives.
  • Trying to land on values in one pass. The two-pass method matters — the traps don't surface until you read the list with fresh eyes.
  • Wordsmithing too early. Get to 3–7 concepts first. Then polish the wording, ideally over the next week with the team.
  • Too many. More than 7 and they stop being non-negotiable. Most great cultures land at 4 or 5.
Coach’s tip
Once defined, put the values into your hiring process. Score every candidate 1–5 on each value (the People Test). Hire only the ones who score 4–5 across the board, and the culture compounds. Hire one 2 and a team of 4–5s starts spending its time managing them instead of building the business.
